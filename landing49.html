Unit 1

Definition

Cambridge dictionary definition is "the possibility of something bad happening" (Cambridge dictionary, No Date). Hubbard (2021) definition has evolved to include that "risk is everywhere". This can more specific when considering cyber security. When considering cyber security risk is all about probability. For example, the probability of exposure to attacks such as firewall attacks and how this could result in loss for the company in regard to a data breach and thus affecting profits (Tunggal, 2022). Most common cyber risks can be split into six categories: Nation states, cyber criminals, hacktivist's, insiders and service providers, developers of substandard products and services and poor configuration of cloud services (Tunggal, 2022). When companies are considering risks, they need to first consider what information if valuable to outsiders and thus build a cyber risk profile (Tunggal, 2022). Companies also need to consider reputational harm if they have a cyber-attack (Securityscorecard, 2021). For example, Facebook has had multiple data breaches which has resulted in a decline in the number of users (Dias, 2021). When building a cyber risk profile three components have to be considered:

1. Threat

2. Vulnerability

3. Components

First of all, companies have to complete a cyber security risk assessment (Cypress, 2020). This helps identify and qualify the valuable parts of the data that may be used and starts the process to create protocols that need to be followed (Cypress, 2020). By having protocols this mitigates the threats and thus creates a risk management protocol for companies. A process to quantify and qualify risks include the OpenFAIR framework. The process can be followed through a flowchart found in figure 1 (Open group guide, 2018). Another example is the NIST framework which can be seen in figure 2 (Impact makers, 2022). These processes help qualify and quantify the risks that companies face to cyber-attacks.

Common security and risk standards are STRIDE, DREAD, Attack Trees and Attack libraries. Attack trees are commonly used in systems which have many different levels that need protection. An example of an attack tree can be found in figure 3 (Santini, 2011). I find that Attack trees give a methodological approach to understanding the security of systems. Having it in a tree process also makes it easy to understand (Schneier, 1999). I have found it difficult to always understand when to use nodes "AND" and "OR" and often find when the system is very large Attack trees can be difficult read as have so many branches (Attack tree threat modelling, 2019).
