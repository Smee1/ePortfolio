The fourth industrial revolution is this evolving aspect in industry moving towards automation (TWI, No date). By using technologies including Internet of Things (IoT), cloud computing and analytics and AI (Rthiraj, 2020). Manufacturers are continuing to evolve to improve their systems. The fourth industrial revolution this has opened up to new risks that previously were not there. Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) discuss the 6 types of risk: technical, competence, acceptance by staff, acceptance by customers, and partners, data privacy and security and financial risks. Highest effect of risk was found in customer channels and revenue stream. Customer channels is the relationship between main criteria and other less specific areas (Kovaitė and Stankevičienė, 2019). One example of Industry 4.0 is cloud computing. Cloud computing is a model for a network to access shared pools of resources (Paul & Ghose, 2012). This can be used on demand with minimal management. Data and analytics are when data is analysed and thus interpreted. By interpreting past data, future markets can be better understood (MBN, 2022). Data security risk is one of the most growing areas of risk in industry 4.0. Data security is how most industries protect digital information from attackers. This can be done via data masking, data erasure, encryption, and data resiliency (IBM, No date). Behavioural risk is due to poor workforce understanding on risk and thus make mistakes leading to data breaches (PwC, No date). Organizations need to ensure they provide adequate training and monitor this with reports, testing, interviews, surveys and observations to ensure behavioural risk is low. Although Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) article had limitations there is an overall consensus about the type of risks found in industrial 4.0 and how to go about assessing, evaluating and protecting against them (Lezzi, Lazoi & Corallo, 2018 & Radanliev et al., 2020).

Risks of Digitalisation of Business Models – A Collaborative Discussion

The Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR), also known as Industry 4.0, refers to the marriage of physical assets and advanced digital technologies – the internet of things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), robots, drones, autonomous vehicles, 3D printing, cloud computing, nanotechnology, and more – that communicate, analyse, and act upon information, enabling organisations, consumers, and society to be more flexible and responsive and make more intelligent, data-driven decisions. (Deloitte Insights, 2020)

As the world today accelerates towards the adoption of the fourth industrial revolution (4IR) technologies, with emerging technologies at our disposal such as cloud computing, automation, Internet of things (IoT), big data, advanced data analytics, robotics, artificial intelligence (AI), unmanned vehicles (UV), augmented reality, and 3D printing to name a few.

These technologies have changed our everyday lives and subsequently lead small, medium, and big corporates to transform their business models. For example, The Internet of and for things will allow us to connect almost anything to the internet with the use of sensors, everything will be smart, enabling greater communication and data-driven services based on increased analytics capabilities. Another example is Big Data Automation and Analytics technology, which will help organisations in analysing a large volume of data and having automated decision-making to provide quicker and real-time services. the This puts the article by Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) in context when referring to Industry 4.0.

Given the advanced technologies presented by Industry 4.0, it is imminent that the risk universe will expand significantly in reflection of the 4IR advancements.

Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) have created and introduced 6 types of risks RADi (Risk assessment for Digitalisation) to be used when planning and implementing Industry 4.0 pillars into existing or new business models. These categories mainly consist of technical risks, competence risks, internal behavioural risks (related to staff skills and attitudes), external behavioural risks (related to customers’ and partners’ attitudes and changes), data security risks, and financial risks.

Some of the Risks that can be realised competence risks leading to job losses for unskilled labour as these technologies will render some jobs obsolete due to automated processes and can also in turn introduce new jobs that never existed before and will not require human intervention, another risk which can be categorised under data security risks will be that of privacy encompassing the handling and access to private information and the authenticity and trusting of the huge amount of information available.

Schwab (2016) emphasises similar sentiments as Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) and highlights matching technologies and risks associated with Industry 4.0.

Schwab (2016) further states that:

Today we find ourselves at the beginning of the fourth industrial revolution, the world is fast changing, hyper-connected, ever more complex and becoming more fragmented but we can still shape our future in a way that benefits all…The fourth industrial revolution has the potential to robotize humanity, and thus compromise our traditional sources of meaning - work, community, family, identity.

References

Kovaitė, K. and Stankevičienė, J. (2019) Risks of digitalisation of business models.

Schwab, K. (2016) The Fourth Industrial Revolution. World Economic Forum.

Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) provides a way to capture the principal characteristics so that companies can assess their vulnerability. There are issues with CVSS due to its lacking formally and empirically justification (Spring et al., 2021a). Spring et al. (2021) criticise CVSS on multiple levels; lack of justification, scores severity not security risk, does not take into account context, does not take into account consequences and mis-score vulnerabilities noted. Spring et al (2021a) do make some suggestions on how CVSS could be improved for future.

1. Conduct user studies with trained sociologists

2. Have a robust empirical scoring system which is transparent

3. Have two scorer agreement for reliability

Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization (SSVC) is a newer system compared to CVSS and tries to tackle the above issues noted (Spring et al., 2021a). I do agree with the authors (Spring et al., 2021a) comments on CCVS and the need for change. I am not sure SSVC meets all that criteria and I think there still are areas of improvement needed for creating a scoring system. SSVC should replace CVSS. SSVC is superior as will help predict which vulnerabilities will be commonly exploited by predicating the risk prioritization method. SSVC creates decision trees where nodes are part of the decisions needs to be made and branches give the various options (Spring et al., 2021b). CVSS has low user satisfaction. EPSS is another example of software which predicts vulnerabilities on a machine-learning classifier. VPR is based on threats and is more technical when checking the severity of vulnerability in a network. SVCC seems to be the most reliable and has high user satisfaction (Spring et al., 2021b).

Changing the Common Vulnerability Scoring System by Jonathan Ajodo

It is now nearly hard for organisations to track and quickly fix vulnerabilities due to the rise in reported vulnerabilities annually. Hence, most organisations focus on the highest priority vulnerabilities based on the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) to prioritise remediation efforts. However, Spring et al (2021) opined that the CVSS scoring algorithm is not justified, either formally or empirically. It was argued that the CVSS formula is not adequately justified because the qualitative responses to questions are converted to ordinal data and assign relative priority rankings which would not give an objective outcome. Spring et al (2021) further argued that CVSS scores severity, not security risk. According to Howland (2021), the lack of justification for the underlying formula and other limitations, the CVSS would not provide a meaningful metric for describing a vulnerability. I agree with this view. This more so that organisations appear to be interested in the risk that a vulnerability or flaw poses to them or how quickly they should react it.

Spring et al (2021) recommended the Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization (SSVC) for prioritising vulnerabilities because it avoids the weaknesses in the CVSS. The SSVC aims to avoid one-size-fits-all solutions in favour of a modular decision-making system with clearly defined and tested parts that vulnerability managers can select and use as appropriate to their context (Spring, et al., 2021). The framework is focussed on decisions and the stakeholders making them rather than technical severity. The use of decision tree to prioritise vulnerabilities in SSVC addresses the lack of transparency in CVSS formula. Overall, vulnerability management involves several stakeholders (with different objectives) prioritising vulnerabilities, as such, the CVSS with its inherent limitations is considered inadequate.

References

What characteristics of CVSS do the authors criticise? Do you agree with the critique? Justify your answer with academic references.

CVSS is designed to identify the technical severity of a vulnerability. What people seem to want to know, instead, is the risk a vulnerability or flaw poses to them or how quickly they should respond to a vulnerability. If so, then either CVSS needs to change, or the community needs a new system. With equal weights given to confidentiality, integrity, and availability, CVSS was created to account for how vulnerabilities affect conventional IT systems. However, in other circumstances, such as when it comes to financial systems or personal user data, data loss might be more serious than losing control of the device. Other situations, such safety-critical embedded devices used in healthcare and industrial control systems, place a greater emphasis on data availability or integrity. It is possible to change the relative weights of secrecy, integrity, and availability using the CVSSv3.1 Environmental metrics. It is unclear whether the Environmental metrics offer a solid and sufficient remedy, though, as this change is mediated by the CVSS formula, which is opaque and unjustified. Additionally, additional design tenets for conventional IT flaws may be incompatible with flaws in other fields. (J. Spring, E. Hatleback, A. Householder, A. Manion and D. Shick, March-April 2021) The authors also discuss a number of alternatives to CVSS. Select one of these alternatives and post an argument for why it should replace CVSS.

Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization (SSVC)

SSVC uses a decision-tree method that can be used by vulnerability management teams as well as developers. instead of putting a dollar value on vulnerabilities, it uses a cost-benefit analysis. {Kitty Kioskli, Nineta Polemi, 2022)

Vulnerability prioritization (VPR)

Combines the risk of exploitation with the technical CVSS rating. examines a number of different types of intelligence to get a score that is more comprehensive. decreases the quantity of High and Critical vulnerabilities to ease the burden of patching. (Muhammed Fatih Bulut, Abdulhamid Adebayo, Daby Sow, Steve Ocepek, 2022)
